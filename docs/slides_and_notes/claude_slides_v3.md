## Slide 3 — What Data Science Means Here

**Not a technical specialism. A scientific judgment competence for brand and communication.**

In the SPARKS context, Data Science is the methodological backbone that lets communication professionals:

- **Read evidence** — distinguish a real signal from a vanity metric
- **Question claims** — recognize when "the data says X" actually means something else
- **Design measurement** — know what counts, what doesn't, and why
- **Assess veracity** — judge whether data sources, methods, and chains of reasoning are trustworthy enough to act on
- **Communicate uncertainty** — translate analysis honestly to clients and audiences

The goal is not to produce data scientists. It is to produce communication professionals who cannot be misled by data — and who do not mislead others with it.

*Speaker notes:* "This is the central positioning. Media & Data already covers operational analytics. Information Technology covers the engineering. Data Science as a MIND module sits between them: it is the scientific reasoning layer that all three programs need. Designers need it to question the briefs they are given. Copywriters need it to evaluate the claims they make. Media strategists need it to defend their measurement choices. Data literacy as scientific judgment, not as tooling. The veracity competence in the middle is the connective tissue — it bridges 'can I measure this' and 'can I communicate this' with 'is what I'm measuring trustworthy.'"

## Slide 4 — Learning Outcomes → Three Competency Clusters

The module's five qualification goals organized into three progressive clusters:

**Cluster A — Foundations of Data Reasoning**
*Understand and apply data science fundamentals.*
What is a measurement? What is a model? What questions can data actually answer in communication and brand work?

**Cluster B — Statistical Reading & Analysis**
*Explain analytical methods · apply statistical techniques for interpretation.*
Exploratory analysis, descriptive and inferential statistics, the difference between correlation and causation in campaign and audience contexts.

**Cluster C — Algorithmic Systems & Applied Judgment**
*Use ML to solve problems · process larger datasets and derive insights.*
How recommender systems, targeting algorithms, and generative AI work — and how to evaluate their outputs critically.

*Speaker notes:* "I've regrouped the five module goals into three clusters. Cluster A is conceptual data literacy. Cluster B is statistical reading — the part most communication professionals are missing and need most. Cluster C is critical understanding of the algorithmic systems they already work with daily. For Cluster C specifically: I do not train communication students to build models. I train them to read, evaluate, and challenge the models that surround them. Each of the five weeks has five competency-oriented learning objectives in the standard 'students will be able to …' format, mapped onto these three clusters. Students see them from week one — they know exactly what they need to demonstrate, and when."

## Slide 5 — Five-Week Roadmap

| Week | Theme | Self-study focus | Synchronous touchpoint |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Introduction to Data Science | Data, evidence, the DS workflow in communication | Virtual classroom: kickoff (1h) |
| **2** | Foundations of Data Analysis | EDA on campaign and audience data; visualization | Virtual classroom: methods clinic (1h) |
| **3** | Statistical Methods | Distributions, uncertainty, causation in measurement | Virtual classroom: 2 × statistics surgeries (1h each) |
| **4** | Algorithmic Systems | Targeting, recommendation, generative models | **On-site days 1–2** (16h) |
| **5** | Data in Practice | Cognitive biases in data interpretation; ethics of measurement in communication | Virtual classroom: synthesis & Q&A (30 min) |

*Speaker notes:* "The on-site phase sits at the end of week 4, deliberately positioned as the climax of the module. Students arrive having built foundations, learned to read data, and worked through statistical reasoning on their own. The two on-site days then activate everything they've learned in the most engaging domain for a communication audience — algorithmic systems. Week 3 is the statistical methods week and gets double virtual-classroom support: a surgery mid-week and one at the end, because statistics is where students most often need synchronous help. Week 5 closes with reflection and bias work — the critical distance that lets students place algorithmic systems in ethical context. For a communication audience, the most dangerous data skill gap isn't 'can't run a model' — it's 'can't recognize when data lies.' Confirmation bias in campaign post-mortems, survivorship bias in case studies, Simpson's paradox in audience segmentation."

## Slide 6 — Self-Study Architecture: Built for the SPARKS Format

**Production logic:** asynchronous content authored as modular learning paths, deliverable via the SPARKS AI-avatar format, with clearly defined synchronous and on-site touchpoints.

**Theoretical anchor:** Flipped Classroom 2.0. Asynchronous time covers lower cognitive levels (remember, understand). Synchronous time is reserved for higher levels (apply, analyze, evaluate).

**Each week follows the same four-part loop (≈26h):**

**Input (≈8h)** — Microlearning units of 15–30 minutes: short readings, avatar-narrated explainers, interactive notebooks. Modular, swappable each semester.

**Guided practice (≈10h)** — Worked examples on real communication datasets with immediate feedback. Low-code first; code optional for the Media & Performance path.

**Reflection (≈4h)** — Short prompts asking students to connect the week's content to their home program (design, story, media).

**Checkpoint (≈4h)** — A small deliverable with a mastery threshold: 80% required to advance; freely repeatable until passed.

**Adaptive synchronous support:** the synchronous load is concentrated where it matters most. Week 3 (Statistical Methods) receives two virtual classroom sessions instead of one — recognizing that statistics is where students most often need real-time clarification.

*Speaker notes:* "This is where the module either works or doesn't. The four-part loop, repeated every week, gives students a predictable rhythm. The microlearning format is compatible with the avatar production format — short, scriptable, modular units that can be updated each semester without rebuilding the course. The mastery threshold is borrowed from established distance-learning practice: continuous formative feedback, not pass-fail. Synchronous time is never used for content delivery — it is reserved for the cognitive work where having a teacher actually matters, and it is concentrated where students need it most."

## Slide 7 — Differentiation: Two Learning Paths

SPARKS students arrive from three very different programs. Instead of forcing one format on all of them, the module offers two paths with shared assessment criteria:

**Communication & Design Path**
For students from Design & Arts and Copy & Story.
- Low-code, browser-based tools
- Focus: data critique, visual data representation, evaluating claims, ethics of measurement
- Deliverables emphasize written argument, visual analysis, and audience translation

**Media & Performance Path**
For students from Media & Data and quantitatively-inclined students from other programs.
- Notebook-based, Python ecosystem
- Focus: hands-on analysis of campaign and audience data, performance measurement, attribution logic
- Deliverables emphasize reproducible analysis and measurement design

**Same competencies. Same grading rubric. Different routes to demonstrate them.**

*Speaker notes:* "This is a direct answer to the central MIND challenge at SPARKS. A communication student needs data literacy as critical reading. A media student needs it as operational competence. Forcing both into one format either bores the media students or loses the designers. Two paths, common rubric — both must demonstrate the same five qualification goals, but through different evidence."

## Slide 8 — Assessment Concept

**Two-Lane model**, aligned with the AI Assessment Scale (Perkins et al., 2024).

**Lane 1 — Assured (in-person, no AI)**
On-site presentation at the end of Week 4 Day 2. Live, unaided. Verifies that the student personally understands their own work.

**Lane 2 — Integrated (digital submissions, AI permitted & documented)**
The three written/digital assessments. AI use is explicitly allowed and must be reflected on. Operates at AI Assessment Scale levels 4–5 (AI collaboration, AI exploration).

**Four continuous performance records across the five weeks:**

| # | Week | Format | Lane |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | End of W2 | Short written analysis (EDA on a campaign dataset) | Integrated |
| **2** | End of W3 | Statistical reasoning brief — interpret a measurement claim | Integrated |
| **3** | End of W4 | On-site pitch — present and defend an algorithmic system analysis | **Assured** |
| **4** | End of W5 | Reflection portfolio — bias critique applied to own program | Integrated |

*Speaker notes:* "A Data Science module in 2026 cannot ignore that students will use AI tools — pretending otherwise produces a hidden curriculum. The Two-Lane model, developed at Sydney, solves this honestly. One lane verifies fundamental competence under controlled conditions. The other lane mirrors real professional practice, where AI assistance is routine and the relevant skill is judgment about when and how to use it. Four assessments, four formats, four different cognitive demands. The Week 2 EDA gives me early diagnostic information — who's struggling, who needs targeted help. The Week 3 statistical reasoning brief is the second checkpoint before the on-site phase, so I know what students are bringing into Week 4. The Week 4 on-site pitch is the Assured Lane — live, unaided, this is where I verify they personally understand their work. The Week 5 portfolio closes the module with the critical reflection that connects everything back to their home program."

## Slide 9 — The Two On-Site Days

**The climax of the module: Algorithmic Systems in practice.**

By Week 4, students have foundations, statistical reasoning, and an understanding of how algorithmic systems work. The on-site days are where they apply, critique, and translate.

**Day 1 — Build & Probe**
- Morning: structured walkthrough of a real algorithmic system (recommender, targeting algorithm, or generative model) used in communication practice
- Afternoon: teams probe the system — what does it optimize for? What does it ignore? Where does it fail?
- Closing: each team documents one concrete failure mode they identified

**Day 2 — Critique & Translate**
- Morning: peer review across teams; structured methodological critique using bias frameworks
- Midday: discussion — what does "good evidence" mean when an algorithmic system is involved in a creative or media decision?
- Afternoon: team pitches to a non-technical audience — explain the system, its limits, and a recommendation (Assured Lane assessment)

**The narrative arc:** build → probe → critique → translate.
One system across both days, increasing critical depth.

*Speaker notes:* "Sixteen hours on-site is precious. I use them for what asynchronous formats and AI avatars cannot do: collaborative dissection of a real algorithmic system, methodological critique in real time, and the kind of judgment discussions that only work face-to-face. The two days share one system and one narrative — build it up, probe its weaknesses, critique it methodologically, then translate the analysis for a non-technical audience. That final pitch is not decorative. For a communication audience, the ability to explain an algorithmic system honestly to clients and creative colleagues — including what it cannot do — is the core deliverable of this module. The Week 5 bias work then takes the critical thinking developed here and broadens it into a general competence."

## Anticipated Question Prep

Eine Frage, auf die du dich vorbereiten solltest:

**"Why not on-site in Week 3 for statistics, where students need it most?"**

> "Three reasons. First, statistics is best learned in repeated short sessions, not in a two-day intensive — so two virtual classroom surgeries in Week 3 serve that better than a single on-site block. Second, algorithmic systems are where statistics gets applied — and applied judgment is what the on-site format does best. Third, putting the on-site phase at the climax of the module rather than the midpoint creates a clearer arc: build foundations, learn to read data, then activate everything in the most engaging domain for a communication audience."
