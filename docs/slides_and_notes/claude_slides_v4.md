## Slide 3 — What Data Science Means Here

**Not a technical specialism. A scientific judgment competence for brand and communication.**

In the SPARKS context, Data Science is the methodological backbone that lets communication professionals:

- **Read evidence** — distinguish a real signal from a vanity metric
- **Question claims** — recognize when "the data says X" actually means something else
- **Design measurement** — know what counts, what doesn't, and why
- **Assess veracity** — judge whether data sources, methods, and chains of reasoning are trustworthy enough to act on
- **Communicate uncertainty** — translate analysis honestly to clients and audiences

**Concrete example throughout the module:** a campaign performance dataset with audience segments, creative variants, engagement metrics, and a targeting component. Students complete the full data-science cycle on this dataset — question → data → analysis → model → interpretation → decision — every week.

The goal is not to produce data scientists. It is to produce communication professionals who cannot be misled by data, and who do not mislead others with it.

*Speaker notes:* "This is the central positioning. Media & Data already covers operational analytics. Information Technology covers the engineering. Data Science as a MIND module sits between them: it is the scientific reasoning layer that all three programs need. Designers need it to question the briefs they are given. Copywriters need it to evaluate the claims they make. Media strategists need it to defend their measurement choices. The running case — a real campaign dataset — anchors the module in concrete data work, not abstract critique. Students see the same dataset evolve across all five weeks: first as raw data to explore, then statistically, then through an algorithmic system. A campaign dashboard shows rising engagement: is this a real effect, a platform artifact, a targeting bias, a seasonality issue, or a vanity metric? That question is what this module trains them to answer."

## Slide 4 — Learning Outcomes → Three Competency Clusters

The module's five qualification goals organized into three progressive clusters:

**Cluster A — Foundations of Data Reasoning**
*Understand and apply data science fundamentals.*
What is a measurement? What is a model? What questions can data actually answer in communication and brand work?

**Cluster B — Statistical Reading & Analysis**
*Explain analytical methods · apply statistical techniques for interpretation.*
Exploratory analysis, descriptive and inferential statistics, the difference between correlation and causation in campaign and audience contexts.

**Cluster C — Algorithmic Systems & Applied Judgment**
*Use ML to solve problems · process larger datasets and derive insights.*
Students use simple supervised and clustering workflows on the running campaign dataset, interpret their limits, and critically evaluate algorithmic systems in communication contexts.

*Speaker notes:* "I've regrouped the five module goals into three clusters. Cluster A is conceptual data literacy. Cluster B is statistical reading — the part most communication professionals are missing and need most. Cluster C is hands-on ML at the right scope: students do not become ML engineers, but they run a simple supervised model on the campaign dataset, evaluate it, and discuss what it can and cannot do. Each of the five weeks has five competency-oriented learning objectives in the 'students will be able to …' format, mapped onto these three clusters. Students see them from week one — they know exactly what they need to demonstrate, and when."

| **4** | Data Science Studio | Modeling, bias, decision-making on the dataset | **On-site days 1–2** (16h) |
| **5** | Data in Practice | Cognitive biases; ethics of measurement in communication | Virtual classroom: synthesis & Q&A (30 min) |

*Speaker notes:* "The same campaign dataset runs through all five weeks. Week 1: introduction and orientation. Week 2: exploratory analysis. Week 3: statistical reasoning, with doubled virtual-classroom support because statistics is where students most often need real-time clarification. Week 4 is the on-site climax — the Data Science Studio — where students do hands-on modeling, encounter bias in their own work, and defend their judgment. Week 5 closes with reflection and bias work in broader ethical context."

## Slide 6 — Self-Study Architecture

**The self-study phase is not passive video consumption. It is a weekly production loop.**

**Four-part loop, repeated every week:**

→ **Input** — Microlearning units (15–30 min), avatar-narrated, modular and swappable

→ **Guided practice** — Worked examples on the campaign dataset, low-code first

→ **Reflection** — Connect the week's content to the student's home program

→ **Mastery-oriented checkpoint** — Small deliverable with repeatable feedback loops

**Theoretical anchor:** Flipped Classroom 2.0 — asynchronous time for foundational learning, synchronous time reserved for higher-order cognitive work.

**Adaptive synchronous support:** synchronous load concentrated in Week 3 where students need it most.

*Speaker notes:* "The four-part loop, repeated every week, gives students a predictable rhythm. The microlearning format is compatible with the avatar production format — short, scriptable, modular units that can be updated each semester without rebuilding the course. Roughly 8 hours input, 10 hours guided practice, 4 hours reflection, 4 hours checkpoint per week. The checkpoint is mastery-oriented with repeatable feedback — continuous formative diagnostics, not pass-fail. Synchronous time is reserved for the cognitive work where having a teacher actually matters, and it is concentrated where students need it most."

## Slide 7 — Differentiation: Two Learning Paths

SPARKS students arrive from three different programs. The module offers two paths with shared core and shared evidence standards:

**Communication & Design Path**
For students from Design & Arts and Copy & Story.
- Low-code, browser-based tools
- Focus: interpret, critique, visualize, explain

**Media & Performance Path**
For students from Media & Data and quantitatively-inclined students.
- Notebook-based, Python ecosystem
- Focus: reproduce, extend, model, validate

**Shared across both paths:** the same campaign dataset, the same research question, the same evidence standards, and the same requirement to justify evidence quality and communicate uncertainty. Different tool depth, same competencies.

*Speaker notes:* "This is the answer to the central MIND challenge at SPARKS. A communication student needs data literacy as critical reading. A media student needs it as operational competence. Forcing both into one format either bores the media students or loses the designers. Two paths, common core dataset, common evidence standards — both must demonstrate the same five qualification goals, but through different evidence. The fairness logic is explicit: same dataset, same research question, same standards of evidence quality and uncertainty communication. The tool depth differs; the cognitive standard does not."

## Slide 8 — Assessment Concept

**Core principle:** AI is allowed where professional judgment is assessed. AI is restricted where personal competence must be verified.

This translates into two assessment lanes — an established model (Two-Lane assessment, Sydney; AI Assessment Scale, Perkins et al., 2024).

**Lane 1 — Assured (in-person, no AI)**
On-site presentation at the end of Week 4 Day 2. Live, unaided.

**Lane 2 — Integrated (digital submissions, AI permitted & documented)**
Three written/digital assessments with reflective documentation of AI use.

**Four continuous performance records:**

| # | Week | Format | Lane |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | End of W2 | EDA evidence memo (campaign dataset) | Integrated |
| **2** | End of W3 | Statistical reasoning brief | Integrated |
| **3** | End of W4 | On-site pitch — defend an analysis | **Assured** |
| **4** | End of W5 | Reflection portfolio (lighter scope) | Integrated |

*Speaker notes:* "A Data Science module in 2026 cannot ignore that students will use AI tools. The core principle — AI where judgment is assessed, AI restricted where competence is verified — handles this honestly. Four assessments, four formats, four different cognitive demands. The Week 2 EDA gives early diagnostic information. The Week 3 brief is the second checkpoint before the on-site phase. Week 4 on-site pitch is the Assured Lane. Week 5 portfolio is deliberately lighter — reflective synthesis, not a fourth full deliverable. The frameworks I reference are well-established: Two-Lane assessment from Sydney, the AI Assessment Scale from Perkins and colleagues."

## Slide 9 — The Data Science Studio (On-Site)

**One coherent communication-data case across two days. Students analyze, model, critique, and communicate.**

**Day 1 — Explore & Probe**
- Morning: deep analytical and statistical work on the campaign dataset
- Afternoon: teams build a simple model (e.g. targeting or recommendation) and probe its outputs
- Closing: each team documents one concrete failure mode they identified

**Day 2 — Critique & Translate**
- Morning: peer review across teams; methodological critique using bias frameworks
- Midday: discussion — what does "good evidence" mean in a creative or media decision?
- Afternoon: team pitches to a non-technical audience — explain the analysis, its limits, and a recommendation (Assured Lane assessment)

**Example system:** a recommender or targeting workflow for a campaign or content platform. The specific case can be swapped each semester.

**The narrative arc:** explore → model → critique → translate.

*Speaker notes:* "Sixteen hours on-site is precious. I use them for what asynchronous formats and AI avatars cannot do: collaborative analysis with real-time feedback, methodological critique, and judgment discussions that only work face-to-face. The two days share one case and one narrative — explore the data, build something simple, probe its weaknesses, critique it methodologically, then translate the analysis for a non-technical audience. That final pitch is not decorative. For a communication audience, the ability to explain a data-driven decision honestly to clients and creative colleagues — including what the data and the model cannot do — is the core deliverable of this module."

## Slide 10 — Why This Concept Works for SPARKS

**For students** — A clear weekly rhythm. Two paths that respect their program. One coherent campaign case across all five weeks. A module that builds toward an integrated Studio climax.

**For the SPARKS format** — Modular, avatar-compatible self-study with a strong theoretical anchor. Synchronous time concentrated where it matters most. An honest answer to AI in assessment.

**For the institution** — Graduates from all three programs who can read data, analyze it, use simple models, recognize bias and uncertainty, and communicate findings responsibly — the scientific judgment competence that makes communication professionals trustworthy.

## Anticipated Question Prep

**Q: "Why not on-site in Week 3 for statistics, where students need it most?"**

> "Three reasons. First, statistics is best learned in repeated short sessions, not a two-day intensive — so two virtual classroom surgeries in Week 3 serve that better than a single on-site block. Second, the Data Science Studio in Week 4 integrates statistics, modeling, and communication — statistics is verified there, not just in Week 3. Third, putting the on-site phase at the end of Week 4 creates a clearer arc: build foundations, learn to read data, then activate everything in an integrated Studio."

**Q: "Where do students actually do data science, as opposed to critiquing it?"**

> "Every week, on the same campaign dataset. Week 2 they run their own EDA. Week 3 they perform statistical analysis. Week 4 in the Studio they build and probe a simple model. Week 5 they integrate the work into a reflective portfolio. The critique is layered onto continuous hands-on work, not a substitute for it."

**Q: "How is the two-path differentiation fair?"**

> "Same dataset, same research question, same evidence standards. Different tool depth. A designer interpreting visualizations and a media student writing Python both have to justify their evidence quality and communicate uncertainty to the same standard. The grading rubric is identical; the route to demonstrating competence differs."
