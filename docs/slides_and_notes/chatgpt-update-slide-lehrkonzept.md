# Module Concept: Data Science
## From Media Data to Decisions
### Applied Data Science for the Media & Data Bachelor

MIND module · Semester 5 · 6 ECTS · 5 weeks

Speaker notes:
Good morning. I would like to present my concept for the Data Science module in the Media & Data Bachelor. My core idea is that this module should function as an integration point in semester five. Students have already encountered AI, coding and prompting, data analytics, predictive modelling, conversion optimization, marketing technologies, and performance metrics. Data Science now brings these strands together into one applied workflow: from a media or marketing question, through data preparation, analysis, statistics and modelling, to a defensible decision.

By semester five, students have already built several foundations:

- Scientific Methods
- Media & Data 1–3
- Artificial Intelligence
- Coding & Prompting
- Data Analytics
- Predictive Modelling
- Conversion Optimization

In semester five, Data Science runs alongside:

- Marketing
- Marketing Technologies
- Performance Metrics
- Creative Media

## Role of the module:
Data Science integrates these strands into one end-to-end analytical workflow.

Speaker notes:
This module is not isolated. It sits at a very important point in the curriculum. Students have already seen data, AI, analytics, coding and modelling in different contexts. The role of Data Science is to consolidate these elements and make them operational: how do I turn a media or marketing problem into a data-science question, analyze the data, build or evaluate a model, and translate the result into a responsible recommendation?

# 3. Core Thesis

## Data Science is the workflow that turns media data into defensible decisions.

Students learn to move through the full cycle:

Question → Data → Cleaning → EDA → Statistics → Modelling → Evaluation → Interpretation → Recommendation

The goal is to train media/data professionals who can:

- run an applied data-science workflow
- understand statistical and model-based evidence
- evaluate uncertainty, bias and validity
- translate findings into media, marketing and platform decisions

Speaker notes:
Media & Data student should experience the full data-science cycle. But the professional target is specific: data-driven decision competence in media, marketing and communication environments.



# 4. Running Case

## One coherent case across five weeks

Example:
A campaign or platform-performance dataset containing:

- audience segments
- creative variants
- impressions, clicks and conversions
- engagement and retention metrics
- channel or placement information
- possible recommendation or targeting logic

Students answer one applied question:

## Which media or marketing decision is justified by the data — and which is not?

Speaker notes:
For the whole course, I use one coherent running case across all five weeks. For example, a campaign-performance or platform dataset. This allows students to see how problem framing, data quality, exploratory data analysis, statistics, modelling and decision communication are connected. The central question is not only “what does the data show?” but also “what decision can we responsibly defend from it?”



# 5. Five-Week Roadmap

| Week | Theme | Core question | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Data Science Workflow for Media & Marketing | What makes a media problem a data-science problem? | Problem framing + data audit |
| 2 | EDA, Data Quality & Feature Construction | What does the data show, hide or distort? | EDA notebook/report |
| 3 | Statistical Reasoning & Experimentation | Which claims are statistically defensible? | Measurement claim brief |
| 4 | Machine Learning for Media Decisions | Can we predict, segment or recommend responsibly? | Model evaluation + live defense |
| 5 | Decision Communication, Ethics & Reproducibility | What should a team or client do next? | Final data story + reflection |

Speaker notes:
The weekly structure follows the logic of a real data-science project. Week one frames the problem and audits the data. Week two is exploratory analysis and feature construction. Week three focuses on statistical reasoning, experimentation, uncertainty and causal pitfalls. Week four introduces machine learning in a practical way: prediction, segmentation, recommendation and evaluation. Week five translates the results into a responsible decision and reflects on ethics, reproducibility and limitations.



# 6. Week 1 — Data Science Workflow for Media & Marketing

Students learn:

- how to translate media and marketing problems into analytical questions
- how to define target variables and outcome metrics
- how to distinguish business questions from data-science questions
- how to assess whether available data can answer the question
- how to identify missingness, bias and measurement problems

Output:
## Problem framing + data audit

Speaker notes:
The first week is about framing. Many data-science failures start before modelling: the question is vague, the target variable is wrong, or the data cannot support the desired conclusion. Students learn to ask: What exactly are we trying to predict, explain or optimize? What is the unit of analysis? What are the relevant metrics? What would count as evidence?



# 7. Week 2 — EDA, Data Quality & Feature Construction

Students work hands-on with the dataset:

- inspect distributions
- detect outliers and missing values
- compare audience segments and channels
- visualize performance patterns
- construct simple features
- distinguish signal, noise and artifacts

Output:
## EDA notebook/report

Speaker notes:
Week two is where students get close to the data. They do exploratory analysis, create visualizations, inspect anomalies and build first interpretations. This is also where they learn that data is not neutral. Platform metrics, tracking systems and campaign dashboards already contain assumptions. Students learn to inspect the data before trusting it.



# 8. Week 3 — Statistical Reasoning & Experimentation

Students learn to evaluate claims such as:

- “Creative A performs better than Creative B.”
- “This audience segment converts more strongly.”
- “The campaign improved engagement.”
- “The new targeting logic caused better performance.”

Methods:

- descriptive statistics
- uncertainty and confidence intervals
- hypothesis-testing logic
- A/B testing
- correlation vs. causation
- Simpson’s paradox and segmentation pitfalls

Output:
## Statistical reasoning brief

Speaker notes:
Week three is the statistical core. The aim is not abstract statistics, but defensible interpretation. Students learn when a difference is meaningful, when a claim is overinterpreted, and why causal language is dangerous in media analytics. This is highly relevant for campaign evaluation, performance metrics and conversion optimization.



# 9. Week 4 — Machine Learning for Media Decisions

Students apply or evaluate simple ML workflows:

- train/test split
- baseline models
- classification or regression
- segmentation or clustering
- recommendation logic
- model evaluation metrics
- feature importance or explainability
- overfitting and generalization

Output:
## Model evaluation + on-site defense

Speaker notes:
Week four makes the module clearly data-scientific. Students use or evaluate simple models, but always with a media decision in mind. The point is to understand what the model predicts, how well it performs, what metric is appropriate, where it fails, and whether its output should influence a real decision.



# 10. Week 5 — Decision Communication, Ethics & Reproducibility

Students synthesize the full workflow:

- What does the analysis support?
- What does it not support?
- What are the limitations?
- Which decision is justified?
- What should be communicated to a client, team or stakeholder?
- How was AI used?
- Is the workflow reproducible?

Output:
## Final data story + reflection

Speaker notes:
The final week turns analysis into professional communication. Students must explain their result honestly: not only what they found, but how reliable it is, what assumptions it depends on, and what should happen next. This connects Data Science to the professional reality of Media & Data: making recommendations under uncertainty.



# 11. Self-Study Architecture

## The self-study phase is active.

Each week follows a production loop:

1. Input
Short avatar-compatible explainers, readings and examples.

2. Guided practice
Hands-on work with media and marketing datasets.

3. Applied task
Notebook, brief, dashboard, model evaluation or data story.

4. Feedback checkpoint
Repeatable task with formative feedback.

Speaker notes:
Because 130 of 150 hours are self-study, the digital phase must be carefully designed. I would not use it mainly for long lectures. Each week students produce something: a data audit, an EDA report, a statistical brief, a model evaluation or a final data story. The asynchronous material provides structure; the tasks create active learning.



# 12. Synchronous and On-Site Design

## Virtual classroom: targeted support

- Week 1: kickoff and workflow orientation
- Week 3: statistics surgery 1
- Week 3: statistics surgery 2
- Week 5: synthesis and Q&A

## On-site phase: Data Science Studio

Two days in Week 4:

- collaborative model evaluation
- peer review
- methodological critique
- live defense of the analytical workflow

Speaker notes:
The synchronous time is used where students need interaction most. Statistics gets extra support because this is often the hardest conceptual part. The two on-site days are positioned in week four because by then students have already framed the problem, explored the data and worked through statistical reasoning. The presence phase can therefore be used for integrated application: modelling, critique and defense.



# 13. Two On-Site Days: Data Science Studio

## Day 1 — Analyze, Model, Evaluate

Morning:
- dataset walkthrough
- data quality and feature discussion
- baseline model logic

Afternoon:
- model evaluation in teams
- metric choice
- error analysis
- failure modes and bias risks

Output:
one model/evidence risk per team

## Day 2 — Defend, Critique, Translate

Morning:
- peer review of analytical workflows
- critique of assumptions and limitations

Midday:
- discussion: When is a model good enough to inform a media decision?

Afternoon:
- live defense to a non-technical audience
- recommendation, uncertainty and limitations

Speaker notes:
The on-site studio is the climax of the module. Students defend their analytical workflow: problem framing, data preparation, statistical assumptions, model choice, evaluation metrics, limitations and recommendation. This verifies personal competence and trains the professional ability to explain data work under questioning.



# 14. Assessment Concept

## Principle:
AI is allowed where professional judgment is assessed.
AI is restricted where personal competence must be verified.

| Week | Assessment | What it tests |
|---|---|---|
| 1/2 | Data audit + EDA notebook/report | data quality, visualization, exploratory reasoning |
| 3 | Statistical reasoning brief | uncertainty, testing, causality, KPI interpretation |
| 4 | Model evaluation + live defense | prediction, segmentation, validation, limitations |
| 5 | Final data story / decision memo | recommendation, ethics, reproducibility, communication |

Speaker notes:
The assessment follows the official idea of continuous performance records. It also reflects the reality of AI-assisted work. Students may use AI in digital submissions, but they must document how. The live defense is the assured component: students must personally explain what they did, why it is valid, and where the limits are.



# 15. Technical Depth

## Required for all students

- work with structured datasets
- perform EDA
- interpret statistical claims
- use or evaluate simple ML workflows
- understand model evaluation metrics
- communicate uncertainty and limitations

## Extension for stronger students

- compare models
- tune features
- implement additional validation
- improve reproducibility
- add explainability
- create a more advanced dashboard or notebook

Speaker notes:
I would not split the module into separate programme paths. Since the focus is Media & Data, there should be one shared workflow. But within that workflow, students can work at different levels of technical depth. Everyone must demonstrate the full data-science reasoning cycle. Stronger students can go further technically.

# Anticipated Questions

## Is this still a MIND module if it is so applied?

Yes. The MIND contribution is methodological: evidence, validity, statistics, modelling, uncertainty and responsible interpretation. The application context is media and marketing, but the intellectual core is scientific reasoning with data.

## Why not make it even more technical?

Because the professional target is Media & Data, not ML engineering. The module should be technically concrete, but the core competence is applying data science to media decisions: framing, analysis, modelling, evaluation and communication.

## What if students have uneven coding skills?

The module uses one shared workflow with adjustable technical depth. Everyone completes the full reasoning cycle. Stronger students can extend the technical implementation, but all students must justify their evidence and defend their recommendation.

## How do you verify understanding when AI tools are allowed?

Through the live on-site defense. Students may use AI for digital work if documented, but they must personally explain their data, methods, model choices, assumptions, limitations and recommendation.

## Why is the on-site phase in Week 4?

Because students first need problem framing, EDA and statistical reasoning. The on-site phase then becomes an integrated Data Science Studio where they apply, critique and defend the workflow.
