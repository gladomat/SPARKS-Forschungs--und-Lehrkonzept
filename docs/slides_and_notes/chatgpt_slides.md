
# Module Concept: Data Science
## Data Science as Scientific Judgment for Communication Professionals
MIND module · 5th semester · 6 ECTS · 5 weeks

[Name · Date · Professorship Data Science]

Speaker notes:
Good morning. I would like to present my concept for the MIND module Data Science. My core idea is simple: for SPARKS, Data Science should not be a miniature computer science course. It should be a judgment module for future communication professionals. Students learn to work through the data-science cycle on communication datasets — but the real outcome is evidence competence: they can question data, analyze it, use simple models, recognize uncertainty and bias, and communicate findings responsibly.



# 1. Module at a Glance

| Dimension  | Design choice                                                       |
|------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Type       | MIND · mandatory · cross-program                                    |
| Audience   | Design & Arts · Copy & Story · Media & Data                         |
| Workload   | 6 ECTS · 150 hours                                                  |
| Format     | 130h self-study · 16h on-site · 4h virtual classroom · office hours |
| Duration   | 5 weeks                                                             |
| Assessment | 3–4 continuous performance records                                  |
| Core case  | Communication data: campaign, audience, content, platform metrics   |

Speaker notes:
The module constraints are clear: five weeks, 150 hours, and most of the learning happens outside the classroom. That means the module cannot rely on classical lecturing. It needs a strong self-study architecture, precise learning tasks, and a presence phase that is used for what cannot be replaced by videos or AI avatars: discussion, critique, application, and defended judgment.



# 2. What Data Science Means Here

## Not a technical specialism.
## A scientific judgment competence for brand and communication.

Students learn to:

- read evidence, not just dashboards
- distinguish signal from vanity metrics
- design meaningful measurement
- apply basic data analysis and statistical reasoning
- use simple ML workflows critically
- assess data quality, bias, and uncertainty
- communicate findings without overstating them

Example:
A campaign dashboard shows rising engagement. Students learn to ask:
Is this a real effect, a targeting artifact, seasonality, platform bias, or simply a vanity metric?

Speaker notes:
This is the central positioning. SPARKS students will work in environments saturated with dashboards, AI tools, platform metrics, and algorithmic claims. The danger is twofold: some students may avoid data because it feels too technical, while others may trust data too quickly because dashboards look objective. This module teaches the middle competence: using data without becoming naïve about it.



# 3. Learning Outcomes as Three Competency Clusters

## A. Foundations of Data Reasoning
Students understand what data, measurement, variables, datasets, models, and evidence mean in communication contexts.

## B. Statistical Reading & Analysis
Students conduct exploratory analysis, interpret distributions, understand uncertainty, and distinguish correlation from causation.

## C. Algorithmic Systems & Applied Judgment
Students use simple ML workflows and evaluate algorithmic systems such as targeting, recommendation, and generative AI.

Core principle:
Students do not become ML engineers.
They become communication professionals who can analyze data, challenge claims, and defend evidence-based decisions.

Speaker notes:
The official qualification goals include foundations, data analysis, statistics, machine learning, large datasets, and practical applications. I organize these into three progressive clusters. The students first learn how to reason with data, then how to analyze and interpret it, and finally how to evaluate algorithmic systems in real communication settings.



# 4. Five-Week Roadmap

| Week | Theme | Main student output |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Data, Evidence & Communication | Measurement critique |
| 2 | Exploratory Data Analysis | EDA evidence memo |
| 3 | Statistical Reasoning | Measurement claim brief |
| 4 | Data Science Studio | Live algorithmic-system pitch |
| 5 | Bias, Ethics & Responsible Communication | Reflection portfolio |

Synchronous design:
- Week 1: virtual kickoff
- Week 3: two statistics surgeries
- Week 4: two on-site studio days
- Week 5: synthesis and Q&A

Speaker notes:
The module follows a clear arc. Week 1 establishes data as evidence. Week 2 makes students work hands-on with exploratory analysis. Week 3 focuses on statistical interpretation, because this is where many students struggle. Week 4 is the presence-phase climax: a Data Science Studio where students analyze, model, critique, and communicate one coherent case. Week 5 closes with bias, ethics, and responsible communication.



# 5. The Running Case

## One coherent case across the module

Example case:
A campaign performance dataset with:

- audience segments
- creative variants
- platform metrics
- engagement and conversion indicators
- possible recommendation or targeting logic

Students work through the full cycle:

Question → Data → Analysis → Model → Interpretation → Decision → Communication

Speaker notes:
A running case prevents the module from becoming a collection of disconnected methods. Students repeatedly return to the same professional situation: a communication team must make a decision based on imperfect data. This lets them experience the full data-science reasoning cycle without pretending that all students need to become technical specialists.



# 6. Self-Study Architecture

## The self-study phase is not passive video consumption.

Each week follows a four-part production loop:

1. Input
Short avatar-compatible microlearning units, readings, and examples.

2. Guided practice
Hands-on work with real or realistic communication datasets.

3. Reflection
Short prompts connecting the week to Design, Story, or Media practice.

4. Checkpoint
Repeatable mastery-oriented task with feedback.

Speaker notes:
The self-study phase carries most of the workload, so it must be designed as a learning production system. Students do not just watch content. They produce evidence every week: a critique, an analysis, a brief, a visualization, or a reflection. The format is compatible with SPARKS’ semi-virtual model because the content is modular, scriptable, and updateable.



# 7. Differentiation: Two Learning Paths

## Same competencies. Same evidence standards. Different tool depth.

### Communication & Design Path
For Design & Arts and Copy & Story students.

Focus:
- low-code tools
- data critique
- visual data representation
- claim evaluation
- audience translation

### Media & Performance Path
For Media & Data and quantitatively inclined students.

Focus:
- notebooks and Python
- reproducible analysis
- performance measurement
- simple modeling
- validation and interpretation

Shared core:
same case, same research question, same rubric.

Speaker notes:
The MIND challenge is heterogeneity. Designers, writers, and media/data students do not need identical tool paths, but they do need shared evidence standards. The two-path model respects different entry points without lowering expectations. A design student may demonstrate competence through critique, visualization, and translation. A media/data student may demonstrate it through reproducible analysis and modeling. Both must justify evidence quality and communicate uncertainty.



# 8. Assessment Concept

## Principle:
AI is allowed where professional judgment is assessed.
AI is restricted where personal competence must be verified.

| Week | Assessment | Mode |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | EDA evidence memo | AI permitted, documented |
| 3 | Statistical reasoning brief | AI permitted, documented |
| 4 | Live algorithmic-system pitch | On-site, unaided defense |
| 5 | Reflection portfolio | AI permitted, reflected |

Assessment logic:
- continuous performance records
- different formats
- individual and group elements
- live verification of understanding
- explicit AI documentation

Speaker notes:
A Data Science module in 2026 cannot ignore AI. My assessment model separates two questions. First: can students use AI and data tools responsibly in realistic professional work? Second: do they personally understand what they are submitting? Digital submissions allow AI use, but it must be documented and reflected. The on-site pitch verifies personal competence through live explanation and defense.



# 9. Two On-Site Days: Data Science Studio

## Day 1 — Analyze & Probe

Morning:
- guided walkthrough of the case dataset
- data quality, variables, missingness, bias

Afternoon:
- exploratory analysis and simple model/prototype
- what does the system optimize for?
- what does it ignore?
- where can it fail?

Output:
one documented failure mode or evidence risk per team

## Day 2 — Critique & Translate

Morning:
- peer review across teams
- methodological critique using bias and evidence frameworks

Midday:
- discussion: What counts as good evidence in algorithmic communication systems?

Afternoon:
- live team pitch to a non-technical audience
- explain system, limits, uncertainty, and recommendation

Narrative arc:
Analyze → Probe → Critique → Translate

Speaker notes:
The two on-site days are used for the highest-value learning activities: collaborative analysis, real-time critique, and defended communication. The point is not just to build something. The point is to understand what the analysis can and cannot support. The final pitch is therefore not decorative. It tests the core competence of the module: can students translate data and algorithmic evidence responsibly for a communication decision?



# 10. Why This Works for SPARKS

## For students
A clear rhythm, concrete cases, differentiated paths, and practical relevance for communication work.

## For the SPARKS format
Modular self-study, avatar-compatible content, targeted synchronous support, and a high-value presence phase.

## For the institution
A Data Science module that strengthens the MIND profile: scientific foundations, applied judgment, responsible AI, and communication-specific evidence competence.

Closing sentence:
Graduates should be able to use data without becoming naïve about it — and communicate evidence without overstating it.

Speaker notes:
This concept rests on four design choices. First, Data Science is framed as scientific judgment for communication professionals. Second, students work hands-on through a full data-science reasoning cycle, but at an appropriate level of technical depth. Third, the module respects heterogeneous student backgrounds through two learning paths with shared standards. Fourth, assessment treats AI honestly while still verifying personal competence. That is why I believe this module fits SPARKS: it is scientific, applied, communication-specific, and realistic for the semi-virtual format.


# Anticipated Questions

## Why not make this a Python-heavy course?

Because the module is compulsory and cross-program. Media & Data students should have a technical path, but Design & Arts and Copy & Story students need data competence as judgment, critique, and communication. The shared goal is evidence competence, not uniform coding depth.

## Why place the on-site phase in Week 4?

Because the presence phase should activate prior learning. By Week 4, students have already worked with data, EDA, and statistical reasoning. The on-site studio then integrates these elements into applied algorithmic judgment.

## How do you ensure students really understand their AI-assisted submissions?

Through the live on-site pitch and defense. AI can support digital work, but students must personally explain the data, method, uncertainty, and recommendation.

## How do you avoid overloading non-technical students?

By using a shared case but differentiated tool paths. The Communication & Design path emphasizes interpretation and translation; the Media & Performance path adds more reproducible analysis and modeling. Both are assessed against the same evidence standards.
